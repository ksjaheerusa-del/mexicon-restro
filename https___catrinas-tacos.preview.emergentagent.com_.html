    What is a catrina in Mexican culture?
    An AI Overview is not available for this searchCan't generate an AI overview right now. Try again later.
    AI Overview
    A Catrina in Mexico is a famous, elegantly dressed female skeleton figure, an iconic symbol of the Día de los Muertos (Day of the Dead) holiday, representing the idea that death comes for everyone, rich or poor, and is celebrated as part of life, not feared. Originally a satirical cartoon by José Guadalupe Posada mocking vanity, she was popularized by Diego Rivera and is now seen in elaborate costumes, makeup, and decorations during the festivities, often with a male counterpart, the Catrin.
     
    Origins and meaning
    • Satirical beginnings: Posada created "La Calavera Garbancera" (The Garbancera Skull) in the early 1900s as a critique of Mexicans who adopted European styles, reminding them that death equals everyone out, says the PBS article. 
    • Name change: Artist Diego Rivera later dressed her in elegant French attire and named her "La Catrina," solidifying her place in Mexican culture, notes the EL PAÍS article. 
    • Symbol of equality: She represents mortality, social class, and the Mexican view of death as a natural part of the life cycle, not something to be feared, says the muertolandia.com article. 
    Modern Day of the Dead
    • Iconic symbol: La Catrina is now the face of Día de los Muertos, appearing on sugar skulls, altars (ofrendas), and in parades, notes the museum website. 
    • Costumes: People dress as Catrinas and Catrins with skull makeup, ornate hats, and fancy clothing, celebrating death with beauty and humor, says the Zinnia Folk Arts article.
